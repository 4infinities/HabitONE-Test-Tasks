Generated: 2026-05-21  |  Experiment start: July 23, 2021  |  Platform: Mobile only

1. Executive Summary

Recommendation: DO NOT LAUNCH

The new payment screen UI produced no statistically significant improvement on any primary business metric. The primary metric — payments per user (total successful payments per person, capturing both conversion and repeat purchases) — was 0.1122 in the test group vs 0.1152 in control (difference: -0.0029, p=0.731 — not significant, and the direction is negative). Average Revenue Per Payer was $644 (test) vs $491 (control), also not significant (p=0.131). There is no statistical evidence that launching would increase payments or revenue.

2. What Was Tested and Why

The product team redesigned the in-app payment screen UI for mobile users to increase successful purchases of in-game currency. Only the screen layout was changed — prices, currency amounts, and game mechanics were unchanged. The experiment ran from July 23, 2021, splitting new registrations into: a control group (original screen) and a test group (redesigned screen).

This game operates on a whale monetization model — a small fraction of high-spending users drives the majority of revenue. The analysis therefore covers overall conversion rate, average payment amount, total revenue per user, and the impact on the whale segment separately.

3. Who Was Included

  • Platform: Mobile users only (the redesign was built for mobile). Non-mobile users were excluded.
  • Registration date: Only users registered on or after July 23, 2021 (experiment start). Pre-existing users were excluded to avoid contamination.
  • Successful payments only (failed transactions excluded from revenue metrics).
  • Final cohort: Control: 6,070 users  |  Test: 5,961 users

5. Statistical Significance — Plain English

Statistical tests used:

  • ★ Payments Per User (primary): Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric; appropriate because the distribution is zero-inflated — most users pay 0 times). Tests whether the test group produces more payments per user than control. P-value: 0.731.
  • Avg Revenue Per Payer: Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric; appropriate because payment amounts are heavily right-skewed due to whale spending). P-value: 0.131.
  • Revenue Per User: Mann-Whitney U test. P-value: 0.730.
All three primary metrics: p-value > 0.05 — no statistically significant effect found. In plain terms: the observed differences between test and control are consistent with random variation. We cannot conclude that the new design changes user behaviour.

95% bootstrap confidence interval for PPU difference (test − control): [-0.0717, +0.0658]. The interval includes zero — the redesign shows no reliable impact on payment frequency.

The experiment may be underpowered for payments per user (required ~670,393 users/group for 5% relative MDE; actual: ~6,070; estimated power: 6%).

  • Country group 1 (majority market, 86% of users): conversion is higher in test. Since group 1 drives most of the user base, this is the largest contributor to any overall effect.
Recommended next step: Run a focused experiment on iOS users in country group 1 to confirm whether the redesign genuinely improves conversion for this segment. If confirmed, a conditional launch (iOS only) may be justified.

9. Risks and Caveats

  • Revenue metric power: Due to extreme variance in payment amounts (Gini ≈ 0.99; a handful of whale users dominate revenue), the experiment would require hundreds of thousands of users to reliably detect a 10% change in RPU. Revenue-based conclusions are therefore underpowered.
  • OS imbalance: A statistically significant difference in device OS mix was found between groups, which may introduce minor confounding.
  • Whale sample size: Only 22–23 whale users per group — not enough for reliable statistical inference on whale-level effects.
  • Country group imbalance: Randomization produced a statistically significant imbalance in country_group (chi² = 18.08, p = 0.0004). Since country groups have meaningfully different baseline conversion rates (2.1% to 5.3%), this imbalance may bias the overall result in either direction.
  • Payment success rate anomaly in country group 4: The test variant produced a 52 pp drop in payment success rate for country group 4 users (15% vs 67%). This is almost certainly a technical bug, not a UX effect — it may have suppressed measured revenue and conversion in the test group, making the new design look worse than it is.
  • One-tailed tests: All tests were designed to detect improvement in test. They are less sensitive to detecting harm (negative effects on control).
  • Novelty effect: Users may behave differently when encountering a new UI for the first time. A longer experiment would reduce this risk.
  • Scope: This analysis covers mobile users only. Impact on other platforms is unknown.

10. Final Recommendation

DO NOT LAUNCH

The new payment screen UI does not demonstrate a statistically significant improvement over the current design on any primary metric. The primary metric — payments per user — was marginally lower in the test group (0.1122 vs 0.1152, p=0.731). Revenue metrics showed no significant change. While whale spending trended upward in the test group, the whale sample is too small to draw firm conclusions.

Important caveat: The overall result is complicated by two anomalies. First, the payment flow for country group 4 appears broken in the test variant (15% success rate vs 67% in control) — this likely suppressed test-group revenue and should be treated as a technical bug. Second, iOS users show a strong positive signal in both conversion and payment success rate. These effects partially cancel in the aggregate, masking what may be a meaningful iOS-specific improvement.

Suggested next steps:

  • Immediately investigate the country group 4 payment flow — the 52 pp drop in payment success rate is almost certainly a technical defect in the new UI's integration with that region's payment gateway. Fix it before any further experiments.
  • Consider a conditional re-test for iOS only — the iOS conversion and success rate signals are strong enough (p < 0.00005) to warrant a dedicated experiment. If a clean iOS-only test confirms the effect, a phased launch for iOS can be justified.
  • Conduct qualitative UX research to understand why the redesign did not lift Android conversion.
  • For future experiments testing revenue metrics, plan for a much larger sample size (the whale model creates extremely high variance that requires large cohorts).
